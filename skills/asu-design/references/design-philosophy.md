# ASU Design Philosophy
> Source: Observational analysis from asu.edu, asu.edu/news-events, and asu.edu/admission.
> Load this file when starting a new ASU project, onboarding to the brand, or when design decisions need to be grounded in intent rather than just rules.

---

## The Core Tension ASU Designs Around

ASU serves an enormous, genuinely diverse audience — first-generation students, transfer students, veterans, international students, parents, researchers, and the general public — all on the same domain. The design system has to work equally hard for a 17-year-old comparing colleges on their phone and a faculty member looking up a colleague's research. That forces every design decision toward **clarity, confidence, and inclusivity** over cleverness or aesthetic novelty.

This is why the brand feels bold rather than refined. Subtlety fails at scale. Gold and maroon cut through because they have to.

---

## Language Tone

**Confident without being aggressive.** States facts at scale without over-explaining or qualifying.

**Inclusive and direct.** "Join an academic community" not "We invite you to consider joining."

**Second person dominant.** The word "you" appears constantly. The UI should feel like it is organizing the user's experience, not presenting ASU's content.

**Short declarative sentences.** Headlines are rarely longer than 10 words. Body copy is functional. No flowery language.

**Action verbs on CTAs.** "Apply now", "Discover what's right for me", "Find your fit." Never passive.

## Design Patterns Observed in the Wild

| Pattern | Implementation |
|---|---|
| Full-bleed hero with single focal image + short headline | Every major page opens this way. Image is always a real person or place — never abstract. |
| Ranked credentials as visual badges | Carousel of badge-style images, not text lists. Badges feel earned, not claimed. |
| Charter quote as design element | "Measured not by whom we exclude…" at display scale. Functions as brand signature. |
| Data presented as typography, not charts | "1 in 3 students are first-generation." Numbers sized as headlines. |
| Adjacency of aspiration and action | Every emotional beat has a paired transactional one. Hero → search tool. Values → CTA. |
| Card grids for stories | Uniform grid with image, headline, "Read more" link. Structure signals abundance. |
| White space is structural, not decorative | Consistent and grid-aligned. Creates clarity, not elegance. |

## The "Why" Behind Visual Choices

| Choice | Why |
|---|---|
| Bold, large headlines | Communicates confidence; works across all screen sizes and ages |
| Gold as primary CTA color | Differentiates from competitors; warm and inviting vs. cold blues in higher ed |
| Real people in all photography | Users identify stock photos and distrust them; authenticity is non-negotiable |
| Charter quote as headline | Positions values (inclusion, access) before credentials |
| Second-person copy | Keeps design user-centered; reduces power imbalance |
| Minimal color variety in UI | Restraint reduces cognitive load across thousands of pages |
| Stats as display typography | Numbers are more credible stated plainly; visualizing implies interpretation needed |
| Action-first CTAs | Respects user time; assumes they already want to engage |

---

## What This Means for How We Design

- **Lead with people.** Real person over abstract visual, always.
- **Don't make the user search for the next step.** Every informational section needs an adjacent action.
- **Write like ASU talks.** Short. Direct. You-focused. No hedging.
- **Numbers earn their own headlines.** Striking stats get space and scale, not paragraphs.
- **Confident, not loud.** Bold hierarchy and high contrast without tipping into noise.
- **Belonging before prestige.** Especially in student-facing contexts, lead with inclusion and access before rankings.
